Setting up a balcony garden sounds complicated until you actually do it. Most people spend more time planning than planting. This guide gives you a realistic two-day schedule designed for Malaysian conditions — high humidity, intense afternoon sun, and the occasional heavy downpour.

## Before you start: three things to check

**1. Which direction does your balcony face?**
North-facing balconies in Malaysia get limited direct sun — stick to shade-tolerant plants like kangkung, mint, and ferns. South-facing gets good morning light. East or west-facing is ideal for tomatoes, chillies, and flowering herbs.

**2. Is there a weight limit?**
Most Malaysian apartment balconies are rated for 150–200 kg/m². A standard 3-tier planter filled with soil weighs roughly 15–20 kg — well within limits. If you plan a large rooftop setup, check with your building management.

**3. Does your condo allow it?**
Most strata properties in Malaysia permit container gardening on balconies. Hanging planters off balcony railings is sometimes restricted — check your house rules first.

## Saturday: Plan and assemble (3–4 hours)

### Morning — Measure and buy

Bring a tape measure to your balcony. Note the width, depth, and ceiling height. You want at least 60 cm of depth to fit a wall planter and still walk past it comfortably.

**What to buy (or compare with a CityGarden starter kit):**
- Vertical wall planter or 3–5 hanging pots
- COCO peat potting mix (avoid garden soil — it compacts and drains poorly in containers)
- Seeds or seedlings: lettuce, kangkung, coriander, and one chilli plant are a solid first set
- A watering can with a gentle rose head
- Liquid seaweed fertiliser (optional but helpful for leafy greens)

### Afternoon — Assemble and fill

1. Mount your wall planter at eye level or slightly above. Use wall plugs rated for at least 10 kg per screw.
2. Fill each pocket or pot to 2–3 cm below the rim. Press the soil down gently — no air pockets, but don't compact it.
3. Water the soil before planting until it drains freely from the bottom. This settles the mix and tells you how fast it drains.

---

## Sunday: Plant and set routine (2–3 hours)

### Morning — Plant

1. **Start with seedlings for the best success rate.** Seeds work, but seedlings from a nursery or hypermarket give you a 2–3 week head start.
2. **Sun-hungry plants go where they get the most light.** Chillies and tomatoes want 5–6 hours of direct sun. Mint and coriander prefer partial shade.
3. **Don't overcrowd.** Lettuce needs 15–20 cm spacing; chillies need 25–30 cm. Overcrowding causes humidity buildup and fungal problems in Malaysia's wet climate.
4. **Water in gently** after planting so roots make contact with the soil.

### Afternoon — Set your watering routine

In Malaysia's heat (average 32–34 °C in Klang Valley), container plants dry out fast. A reliable schedule:
- **Morning watering (6–8 am):** Deep water until it drains from the bottom. This is the most important watering of the day.
- **Evening check (6–7 pm):** Press a finger 3 cm into the soil. If it's dry, water lightly. If it's still moist, skip it.
- **Rain days:** Skip watering entirely — containers collect rainwater efficiently.

**Tip:** A self-watering reservoir, like the one proposed in the CityGarden Self-Watering Upgrade Kit, can simplify this routine by wicking moisture to roots on demand.

## Week one expectations

- **Days 1–3:** Plants look slightly stressed after transplanting. This is normal — they're establishing roots.
- **Days 4–7:** New leaf growth appears. Your first sign that the plant has taken.
- **Day 14 onwards:** Lettuce and kangkung are ready for their first harvest (cut outer leaves, leave the centre to keep producing).

Chillies take 60–90 days to fruit. Think of them as a background project while your leafy greens give you weekly harvests.

---

## The one mistake to avoid

**Overwatering.** It kills more container plants in Malaysia than any other cause. Tropical humidity slows evaporation and roots sitting in waterlogged soil rot quickly. Always check soil moisture before watering, and make sure every container has drainage holes.
